Model Performance Issues to Address
1. High-Confidence Failures Your models are consistently wrong on games where they show high confidence (60-75% win probability). This suggests:
	•	Features may be overweighting recent form or missing key contextual factors
	•	Need to add uncertainty calibration to prevent overconfident predictions
	•	Consider ensemble diversity - your three models agree too often on incorrect predictions
2. Home Ice Advantage Overestimation Many losses occur when predicting home wins with 55-70% confidence. Ask Replit to:
	•	Re-examine home ice advantage calculation (may be using outdated league averages)
	•	Add venue-specific factors (altitude for Colorado, travel distance, back-to-back games)
	•	Weight recent home/away splits more heavily
3. Feature Engineering Gaps Request these additions:
	•	Goalie effects: Starting goalie quality drastically impacts outcomes
	•	Rest differential: Days since last game for each team
	•	Lineup changes: Injuries, call-ups from AHL
	•	Head-to-head history: Division rivals play differently
	•	Score effects: Teams play differently when leading/trailing
	•	Special teams: Power play and penalty kill efficiency
4. Model-Specific Improvements
For the Meta model (currently 56% accuracy based on your data):
	•	It's not adding value over individual models - needs better weighting logic
	•	Try stacking with a secondary classifier instead of simple averaging
	•	Weight models based on game context (Elo works better for mismatches, XGB for close games)
5. Calibration Issues Your probabilities don't match outcomes - a "60% confident" prediction should win 60% of the time. Ask for:
	•	Platt scaling or isotonic regression for probability calibration
	•	Separate calibration for home vs away predictions
	•	Temperature scaling for the ensemble
6. Training Data Suggestions
	•	Exclude shootout results or handle them separately (they're essentially coin flips)
	•	Weight recent seasons more heavily (rule changes, league expansion affect patterns)
	•	Consider within-season regime changes (teams improve/decline during season)
The fact that your ensemble often gets games wrong when all three models agree strongly suggests a systematic bias rather than random error - that's the priority to fix.
